# Open-Earth-Polar-Model
![License: MIT](https://img.shields.io/badge/License-MIT-yellow.svg)
**Welcome to the Open Earth Polar Model Project.**

This is an open-source, collaborative initiative aimed at exploring and developing alternative geophysical and cosmological models outside the constraints of institutional space agencies or closed scientific circles. Our focus is on a **polar-axis-centered, spherical Earth model** that integrates ideas of **time-curvature, rotational asymmetry, and field dynamics**, all grounded in observations accessible to everyday researchers.

## Vision

We believe science should be transparent, accessible, and open to all. This project encourages independent thinkers, engineers, physicists, and curious minds to contribute toward a deeper understanding of Earth’s structure, energy fields, and rotational mechanics.

## Objectives

- Develop a **new Earth model** centered on its **rotational axis**, rather than traditional heliocentric or flat paradigms.
- Explore the implications of **time-curvature relationships** as a replacement for Newtonian gravity in this model.
- Investigate **asymmetries between the North and South poles**, including field strength, climate behavior, and rotational balance.
- Consider Earth's **motion around the Sun** as a curved, wave-like motion rather than a perfect orbit.
- **Democratize geophysics** by allowing everyone to participate in modeling, testing, and refining through open data and community discussion.

## Why This Model?

- Existing models rely heavily on data from space agencies inaccessible to independent researchers.
- Polar-coordinate systems are powerful tools in wave physics and may offer a better framework for understanding Earth's internal and external dynamics.
- Incorporating **rotational and energetic asymmetries** could lead to better modeling of phenomena like field generation, auroras, and magnetospheric behavior.

## Contribution Guidelines

We welcome all respectful contributors, whether you're a scientist, student, or hobbyist.

1. Fork the repo and submit a pull request with any additions or corrections.
2. Open an issue to start discussions or suggest experiments.
3. Submit observational or experimental data — especially independent or historical sources.
4. Help develop mathematical proofs or simulations supporting the model.
